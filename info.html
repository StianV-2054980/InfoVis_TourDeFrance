The Tour de France is one of the most prestigious and challenging bicycle races in the world. It is held annually, primarily in France, although it occasionally passes through neighboring countries. The race typically lasts for 21 days (including 2 rest days), covering approximately 3500 kilometers and consisting of various stages. Riders compete not only for individual stage victories but also for the overall win, known as the General Classification (GC). The overall win is determined by the rider with the lowest cumulative time over all stages. Next to the overall win, there are also other classifications explained below. Each with their own jersey.

Yellow Jersey (Maillot Jaune): Awarded to the rider with the lowest overall time, making them the race leader.

Green Jersey (Maillot Vert): Given to the leader of the points classification, which rewards sprinters who accumulate points by finishing in the top positions of stages and intermediate sprints.

White Jersey (Maillot Blanc): Awarded to the best young rider (under 26) in the General Classification.

Polka Dot Jersey (Maillot à Pois Rouges): Worn by the best climber, who earns points by reaching mountain summits before other riders.

The Tour de France has seen many legendary cyclists who have achieved great success. Here are some of the most famous winners:

Eddy Merckx (Belgium): Known as "The Cannibal," Merckx won the Tour de France five times (1969, 1970, 1971, 1972, 1974). He is often considered one of the greatest cyclists of all time.

Jacques Anquetil (France): Anquetil was the first rider to win the Tour de France five times (1957, 1961, 1962, 1963, 1964). He was known for his time-trialing prowess.

Bernard Hinault (France): Nicknamed "The Badger," Hinault also won the Tour de France five times (1978, 1979, 1981, 1982, 1985). He is the last Frenchman to have won the race.

Miguel Indurain (Spain): Indurain won the Tour de France five consecutive times (1991-1995), a feat only matched by Lance Armstrong, whose seven titles (1999-2005) were later stripped due to doping violations.

Chris Froome (Great Britain): Froome has won the Tour de France four times (2013, 2015, 2016, 2017), making him one of the most successful modern riders in the race.

Tadej Pogačar (Slovenia): Pogačar has emerged as a dominant force in recent years, winning the Tour de France thrice (2020, 2021, 2024) at a young age.
